Surface water floods account for a significant share of flood-related damages, contributing to at least 45% of building damage (Bernet et al., 2017). Impact-based forecasting can support mitigation strategies and strengthen resilience to future events. In this study, we collaborate with a group of stakeholders. Our previous analysis revealed that 26% of swiss campsites have 40–100% of their total area exposed, based on the national surface runoff map
Traditionally, rainfall thresholds for surface water flood warnings are determined using a variety of methods, including: (a) meteorological quantiles (Panziera et al., 2016) , (b) calibration with observed damage data (e.g., insurance claims) (Horton et al., 2025), (c) local user experience and expert knowledge (Meléndez-Landaverde & Sempere-Torres, 2025), and (d) hydrological-hydraulic simulations (Sharifian et al., 2023). However, each of these approaches presents certain limitations. Many are not impact-based, often rely on damage data from buildings that may not adequately represent the specific vulnerabilities of other land uses such as campgrounds and the use of local experiential knowledge is limited in areas where citizens have not previously experienced severe flood events, resulting in gaps in risk perception and preparedness. Furthermore, simulation-based approaches typically involve high computational costs, which can hinder their applicability for real-time decision-making.
To address these limitations, we develop event storylines for impact-based forecasting. We first run LISFLOOD-FP under rainfall-only conditions to derive physically based impact thresholds and counts of affected camping pitches. We then create compound storylines by adding a localized point inflow representing overflow from the adjacent stream, exploring three inflow magnitudes (low/medium/high) treated as scenario assumptions to quantify incremental impacts.
